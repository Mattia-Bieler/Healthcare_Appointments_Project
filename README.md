# Healthcare Appointments Project (SET UP NOT FINISHED)
## Project Background
This project aims to explore and provide recommendations for future analysis to tackle the cost missed appointments with General Practitioners (GPs) have on the National Health Service (NHS). This business problem stems from a breakdown in communication between different departments and a neglect at looking at the data, resulting in decisions being made that are not data-driven. Therefore, while a solution for reducing or eliminating missed appointments is needed, the primary focus at this stage is on whether the NHS has adequate staff and capacity, as well as what the actual utilisation of resources was. Additionally, this report will quantify the number of missed appointments, focusing especially on missed GP appointments.

## Analytical Approach
I initially converted the national_categories file type from Excel to CSV, reducing the importing time. Prior to importing actual_duration.csv, appointment_regional.csv and national_categories.csv, I ensured that the necessary libraries were imported for the project. __Pandas and NumPy__ were imported for analysis, data manipulation, data cleaning and numerical computing. To create visualisations, __Seaborn and Matplotlib__ were imported. The three files, as well as tweets.csv later on, were imported using the code, __df = pd.read_csv(‘file_name.csv’)__, naming the DataFrames ‘ad’, ‘ar’, ‘nc’ and ‘tweets’. These names correspond to their file names, abiding by __PEP8 guidelines__. For each DataFrame, the __dtypes, describe() and info() functions__ were used, providing an overview of the four DataFrames. Additionally, the DataFrames were checked for __missing values and duplicates__. The tweets DataFrame contained 167 missing values. However, as they were presented as ‘NaN’ already, no changes were made to the DataFrame. Furthermore, the ar DataFrame contained duplicates, which were not remove because they only accounted for 3.6% of the DataFrame’s row count.

For the next step, I needed to import the __datetime module__ to change the data type and date format of the appointment date column for the ad and nc DataFrames. This enabled specific calculations to be done and standardised the date format for both DataFrames, allowing for comparisons to be explored clearly.

To enhance the analysis and gain more concrete insight, I incorporated four percentage calculations. These are ‘daily utilisation’, ‘attended appointments per month’, ‘attended GP appointments per month’ and ‘appointments per service setting’. While writing the code for the project, I ensured a logical process was followed that abided by __PEP8 guidelines__ (see appendix). For example, for attended GP appointments, the code is separated into the five individual sections, each with clear comments on what the following code does. Furthermore, the variables used (‘gp_attended’, ‘count_by_month’, ‘total_count_by_month’ and ‘percentage_by _month’) explain the codes functionality on their own. This increases the value of the variables, as it can be reuse with a clear understanding of its purpose.

## Visualisations and Insight
The main logic behind the decision to use barplots and lineplots was that they are easily recognisable graph types that are simple to understand, and both graph types are excellent for comparing various categories. Furthermore, lineplots have the added aspect of comparing these categories over a period. For the coding aspect of the visualisations, I aimed to keep the structure the same. After creating a graph, I improved the visual aspect of the visualisation with appropriate labels, as well as adding a grid, specifying a colour palette, and customising the legend when necessary. Additionally, the axes tick labels were customised, and data points were added when appropriate. As the lineplot code example in the appendix illustrates, the date format was changed to %d %b (e.g., Aug 21), making the visualisation clearer. The barplot code example in the appendix shows that a ‘for loop’ was used to display bar values on the graph. Furthermore, the barplot for words related to healthcare also has an upper and lower threshold to remove overrepresented words and present the top fifteen hashtags.

I have added several extra visualisations to improve the understanding of the data. For example, I created four additional seasonal lineplots that excluded the ‘General Practice’ service setting. This was done because the large number of appointments attributed to ‘General Practice’, which accounts for 91% of all appointments, made it difficult to see the trend of the other service settings across the given periods. A clear pattern exists across all four seasons in which the number of appointments per service setting rise and fall over a period of seven days. This is to be expected as less appointments across the NHS take place at the weekends. Furthermore, four seasonal lineplots looking at the number of daily appointments were also created to help understand the utilisation of NHS resources.

## Patterns and Predictions
![monthly_utilisation_lineplot](https://github.com/Mattia-Bieler/Healthcare_Appointments_Project/assets/132078605/1de311df-888b-4caf-8ce5-50b5ba6e30c8)

Insights drawn from looking at the calculated percentages shows that for the busiest months (October and November 2021), the daily utilisation was at 84% of the maximum capacity. In short, in no given month does the daily utilisation exceed the maximum capacity of 1,200,000 appointments. However, this calculation is done by dividing the number of monthly appointments by thirty (see appendix) and does not factor in that daily appointment numbers differ significantly, which is shown by the four seasonal lineplots for daily appointments. In fact, there are 175 days with more than 1,200,000 appointments (see appendix). A solution could be to increase staff numbers on busy days or spread appointments more equally across the week. However, a further analysis on the impact of this daily fluctuation of appointment numbers is needed to better understand if the NHS has inadequate staff numbers and capacity overall, as well as investigate whether appointment attendance is impacted by those 175 days.

Regarding attended appointments, between 90-93% of appointments were attended each month with the percentage going up to 93-95% for attended GP appointments. As the data highlights, the percentage of missed GP appointments is lower than the total percentage of missed appointments. This suggests that to address the concerns of the stakeholders over the cost of missed GP appointments, a further investigation needs to be carried out into why a small fraction of missed GP appointments is having a significant economic impact. One reason, which would need further analysis to confirm, could be because GP has the highest number of appointments per month (expert for October 2021) for healthcare professional type, meaning missed GP appointments would presumably account for a significant portion of the 7-10% of missed appointments per month. 

![avg_unattended_GP_perc_barplot](https://github.com/Mattia-Bieler/Healthcare_Appointments_Project/assets/132078605/415362fa-7c88-4bb6-b540-6cd95da0b7fa)

One thing that is clear is that the longer the waiting time, the higher the percentage of unattended GP appointments. This observation underscores the importance of investigating and addressing factors contributing to extended wait times, as it appears to be a significant determinant in the rate of missed GP appointments, thereby impacting the overall efficiency and cost-effectiveness of healthcare services.
